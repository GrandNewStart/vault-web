Elliptic Curve

Overview

What is Elliptic Curve Cryptography?

Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC) is a modern approach to public-key cryptography. Instead of relying on the difficulty of factoring giant prime numbers (like RSA does), ECC relies on the complex mathematics of points bouncing along a specific algebraic curve: y² = x³ + ax + b.

Because of the unique geometric properties of these curves, ECC offers **the exact same security level as traditional standards but with much smaller keys**. This results in faster cryptographic handshakes, lower memory usage, and significantly reduced battery consumption.

Analogy

The Billiard Table Metaphor

Imagine a pool table with a very strange, curved shape.

  1. You place a ball at a starting point P.
  2. You hit the ball, and it bounces off the curved walls. You draw a line through its path, bounce it to another point, and repeat this process exactly d times (where d is your secret Private Key).
  3. The final coordinate where the ball stops is your Public Key Q (written as Q = d · P).
  4. Anyone can see the starting point (P) and the ending point (Q), but because of the geometry of the curve, it is mathematically impossible to calculate how many times (d) you bounced the ball.

Efficiency

ECC vs. RSA Key Sizes

The main advantage of Elliptic Curve Cryptography is efficiency. To match the security of standard ECC keys, traditional RSA keys must grow exponentially larger:

ECC Key Size

256 bits
Modern Standard

Equivalent RSA Key

3072 bits
Large & Slow
Fewer CPU Cycles: Storing and processing a 256-bit key requires significantly less computing power than a 3072-bit key.
Less Bandwidth: Handshakes on secure connections use smaller packets, accelerating secure session initialization.
Gold Standard for Mobile: Mobile OS platforms use ECC to encrypt storage and keys inside hardware Secure Enclaves without causing UI lag or battery drain.
